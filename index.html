Elshaddai Muchuwa

Computational Cognitive Science & Government · Franklin & Marshall College
Researcher, CARES Lab

I study how technical systems encode meaning, misrepresent people, and distribute harm. My work brings together AI governance, algorithmic fairness, contextual modeling, and Ubuntu-informed approaches to accountability and design.

1. Centering marginalized communities in system modeling and redesign.
2. Structural analysis — treating context as part of the model, not noise to be removed.
3. Community agency — co-created knowledge, participatory methods, and relational accountability.

Research

My research examines how AI clusters, ranks, and interprets people, and how relational, Ubuntu-informed approaches can reshape governance and design. I focus on algorithmic fairness, harm modeling, contextual inference, HRI, and participatory ethics in computational systems.

  • Undergraduate Honors Thesis — Composite Vulnerability Persona (CVP)
    Modeling relational harm using PCA, mixed-effects regression, counterfactual analysis, and stress-profile simulation within an Ubuntu-informed governance framework.
  • Machine Ethics Research — CARES Lab
    Lead analyst for ACM paper; conducted PCA, mixed-effects modeling, and survey validation across nine indicators of ethical reasoning.
  • Cognitive Neuroscience — VR & Memory
    Designed immersive recall experiment; analyzed narrative responses using Azure sentiment analysis and Python feature extraction.
  • Human–Robot Interaction (Misty)
    Programmed affective robot behaviors in MistyPy; built sentiment dashboard (Flask + Supabase).
  • Social Psychology — AI & Admissions
    Built experiment testing effects of AI-generated admissions recommendations on decisions and reasoning patterns.

Service

A lot of my service work began with a responsibility I felt growing up — if someone helped me take the next step, I had to make sure someone else didn’t walk alone. That’s how I ended up mentoring groups of students in Zimbabwe and Brooklyn, usually twenty or more each year. Some are applying to college for the first time, some are trying to learn how to write about who they are, and most just need someone who treats their goals as real. That’s the Ubuntu part for me — possibility is something we build together.

On campus, I try to be a person younger students — especially Black students and international students — can come to. Not formally, but in the small ways that matter: talking through research ideas, course choices, leadership roles, or how to belong in a space that wasn’t designed with them in mind.

The workshops I run — storytelling, leadership, goal-setting — come from the same place. Once someone realizes their story is worth taking seriously, something changes. Those moments remind me why service shapes everything else I do.
