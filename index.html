Elshaddai Muchuwa

Computational Cognitive Science & Government · Franklin & Marshall College
Researcher, CARES Lab

I study how technical systems encode meaning, misrepresent people, and distribute harm. My work brings together AI governance, algorithmic fairness, contextual modeling, and Ubuntu-informed approaches to accountability and design. I take an Afrocentric relational approach grounded in:

1. Centering marginalized communities in system modeling and redesign.
2. Structural analysis—treating context as part of the model, not noise to be removed.
3. Community agency—co-created knowledge, participatory methods, and relational accountability.

Research

My research examines how AI clusters, ranks, and interprets people, and how relational, Ubuntu-informed approaches can reshape governance and design. I focus on algorithmic fairness, harm modeling, contextual inference, HRI, and participatory ethics in computational systems.

  • Undergraduate Honors Thesis — Composite Vulnerability Persona (CVP)
    Modeling relational harm in high-stakes automation using PCA, mixed-effects regression, counterfactual analysis, and stress-profile simulation within an Ubuntu-informed governance framework.
  • Machine Ethics Research — CARES Lab (F&M)
    Lead analyst for ACM publication; ran PCA, mixed-effects models, and survey validation across nine affective–participatory and perception-based cognitive indicators to evaluate shifts in ethical reasoning.
  • Cognitive Neuroscience of Consciousness — VR & Memory
    Designed immersive recall study; built behavioral tasks; analyzed responses using Azure sentiment analysis and Python-based feature extraction pipelines.
  • Human–Robot Interaction (Misty)
    Programmed affective robot behaviors using MistyPy (Python); implemented touch-event detection, LED and pose states; developed a prototype sentiment dashboard (JavaScript + Flask + Supabase).
  • Social Psychology Research — AI & Admissions
    Built between-subjects experiment testing effects of AI-generated admissions recommendations on decision-making; constructed Qualtrics instruments and ran independent-samples tests, regression, correlational analysis, and assumption checks.

Service

A lot of my service work began with a simple responsibility I felt growing up — if someone helped me take the next step, I had to make sure someone else didn’t walk alone. That’s how I ended up mentoring groups of students in Zimbabwe and Brooklyn, usually twenty or more each year. Some of them are thinking about college for the first time, some are trying to write about who they are, and most just need someone who treats their goals seriously. I try to give them what I needed at their age: honest guidance and the sense that their lives can stretch wider than the limits around them. That’s the Ubuntu part for me — possibility is something we build through one another.

I carry that same approach into my work on campus. A lot of younger students, especially Black students and international students, end up navigating spaces where they feel unseen or unsure of where to begin. I’ve made it a point to be someone they can check in with — about classes, research, leadership roles, or just figuring out how to belong in a place that wasn’t built with them in mind. These conversations aren’t formal mentorship; they feel more like passing on something that was passed to me: room to grow without feeling alone.

The workshops I run — storytelling, leadership, goal-setting — come from the same place. I’ve seen how powerful it is when young people are given a space to name their ambitions out loud. Every time someone realizes their story is worth taking seriously, you can feel something shift. Those moments remind me why I keep doing this. Service isn’t a separate part of my work; it shapes the way I think about research, community, and what responsibility looks like in practice.
