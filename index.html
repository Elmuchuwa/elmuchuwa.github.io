Elshaddai Muchuwa

Computational Cognitive Science & Government · Franklin & Marshall College
Researcher, CARES Lab

I study how technical systems encode meaning, misrepresent people, and distribute harm. My work brings together AI governance, algorithmic fairness, contextual modeling, and Ubuntu-informed approaches to accountability and design. I take an Afrocentric relational approach grounded in:

1. Centering marginalized communities in system modeling and redesign.
2. Structural analysis — treating context as part of the model, not noise.
3. Community agency — participatory methods and relational accountability.

Research

I examine how AI clusters, ranks, and interprets people — and how relational, Ubuntu-informed approaches can reshape governance and design. I focus on algorithmic fairness, harm modeling, contextual inference, HRI, and participatory ethics.

  • Undergraduate Honors Thesis — Composite Vulnerability Persona (CVP)
    Modeling relational harm in high-stakes automation using PCA, mixed-effects regression, counterfactuals, and stress-profile simulation.
  • Machine Ethics Research — CARES Lab
    Lead analyst for ACM publication; ran PCA, mixed-effects models, and survey validation across nine affective and cognitive indicators.
  • Cognitive Neuroscience — VR & Memory
    Designed immersive memory-recall tasks; used Azure sentiment analysis and Python pipelines for feature extraction.
  • Human–Robot Interaction (Misty)
    Programmed affective robot behaviors with MistyPy; developed a sentiment-dashboard prototype using Python, Flask, and Supabase.
  • AI & Admissions — Social Psychology
    Designed between-subjects study testing AI-generated recommendations on decision-making; ran regression, correlational analysis, and instrument validation.

Service

A lot of my service work began from a responsibility I felt growing up — if someone helped me take the next step, I had to make sure someone else didn’t walk alone. That’s how I ended up mentoring students in Zimbabwe and Brooklyn, usually twenty or more each year. Some are thinking about college for the first time, others are trying to write about who they are, and most just need someone who treats their goals seriously. I try to give them what I needed at their age: honest guidance and the sense that their lives can stretch wider than the limits around them. That’s Ubuntu for me — possibility built through one another.

I bring the same energy into my campus work. A lot of younger students — especially Black students and international students — navigate spaces where they feel unseen. I try to be someone they can check in with, whether it’s about classes, research, leadership, or simply figuring out how to belong in a place that wasn’t built with them in mind.

The workshops I run — storytelling, leadership, and goal-setting — come from that same ethic. I’ve seen how powerful it is when young people are given space to name their ambitions out loud. Every time someone realizes their story is worth taking seriously, something shifts. Those moments remind me why I keep doing this.
